see problem

example: see exercise 3.

Codewords: $\{10, 11, 000, 001, 010, 011 \}$
Parameter: $(0, 0, 2, 4)$
Kraft-Mcmillan: $$ K = 0 + \frac{0}{2} + \frac{2}{4} + \frac{4}{8} = 1 $$

This code is complete. Alphabet size $6$, and trivially divides evenly $$ \frac{6-1}{2-1}=5 $$

We will proove that for a complete $b$-nary code, the encoded alphabet of size $m$ has the feature $m - 1 = 0 \pmod{b-1}$

b-nary code: $|T|=b$
alphabet of size m: $|S^n|=m$

Before continuing, note that this does not hold for $b=1$, and is trivial for $b=2$, since any value of $m-1$ is divisible by one. We will consider cases where $b>2$

Since $C: S^n \mapsto T^*$ exists and $K=1$,

$$ K = \sum_i^M\frac{n_i}{b^i} = 1 \\ $$ Where $M$ is the longest codeword in $C$, which exists since the alphabet being encoded has a finite size and $C$ is 1-to-1.
Then

$$ m = \sum_i^M n_i $$ and $$ b^M\sum_i^M\frac{n_i}{b^i} = b^M \\ \sum_i^M n_ib^{M-i} = b^M $$

Since $b = 1 \pmod{b-1}$ and $b^k = 1 \pmod{b-1}$ for positive integers of $k$, this equates to

$$ \sum_i^M n_i = 1 \pmod{b-1} \\ m = 1 \pmod{b-1} \\ m-1 = 0\pmod{b-1} $$ Thus $m-1$ divides by $b-1$ with a remainder of zero
